The RFP tells you who writes the best proposal. It rarely tells you who will perform best on your book. That gap is where most disappointing partnerships begin — and it&#x27;s the cheapest thing in the whole process to fix. The standard questions are fine as far as they go: adjuster headcount, geographic coverage, average cycle time, cost per file, tech stack, SLAs, sample reports. They filter for proposal quality, not operational fit. Here are the questions I&#x27;d add — the ones that tell you how a partner will actually run your files. 1. How does the partner&#x27;s file standard get set? Every claims partner will tell you their standards are high. The useful question is who decides what those standards are. Some calibrate to whatever the carrier seems to accept, and the standard quietly drifts down to whatever passes review. It&#x27;s invisible during deployment and expensive after. Others set their standard independently — photo completeness, scope defensibility, coverage rationale documented in-line — applied the same way regardless of which desk is reviewing. Ask them to walk you through what their QA reviewer looks for. If it&#x27;s specific and independent of carrier preferences, that&#x27;s the operation you want. 2. What is the QA layer actually catching, and how often? Most partners report on submissions and closures. Far fewer report on what their internal QA caught before the file reached your desk. QA that catches gaps, coverage misalignments, and scope errors before submission is doing the work that prevents reopens downstream. QA that rubber-stamps is exporting that work to your desk. Ask how many files QA reviewed last quarter, how many needed revision, and what the common gaps were. The specificity of the answer tells you whether QA is real or a checkbox. 3. How are field adjusters trained, and how is that kept current? Most RFPs ask about certifications and licenses. Far fewer ask how training is refreshed during the working year. Partners who handle complex losses well tend to train mid-year, not just at hire — new regulatory developments, new documentation expectations, new coverage patterns from desks across the industry. Ask about the last training session their adjusters completed. If it&#x27;s specific and recent, the operation is paying attention. 4. What changes operationally during a CAT event? Most partners describe CAT capability in capacity terms — deployable adjusters, geographic reach, surge availability. The useful question is what changes when the storm hits. Do file standards hold under surge, or relax? Does QA continue, or move to sampling? Does communication discipline hold, or does the team go quiet because it&#x27;s busy? Ask for a specific story from a past CAT event — what worked, what didn&#x27;t, what they changed after. Operational specifics separate the partners who&#x27;ve been through it from the ones talking in generalities. 5. Who owns the relationship — a rotating name, or a named point of contact? Most deployments hand the carrier a field adjuster name that changes file to file, and continuity gets rebuilt with every claim. Some partners run a desk adjuster model: a named contact who owns communication, status, and escalation across every file on your book, whichever field adjuster is on the ground. One relationship to manage, not a rotating list. It&#x27;s a structural question about how they operate. Ask it directly. None of these get you a longer proposal. They get you a more useful one — and they surface operational fit while switching costs are still low. The RFP is the cheapest place to learn what actually determines performance. Worth using the space deliberately. What&#x27;s the one question your last RFP didn&#x27;t ask that you wish it had? — Mark Vetrovec CEO, Aspen Claims Service

California keeps raising the bar on wildfire claims — and the file standard that cleared last year doesn&#x27;t always clear this year. The direction of travel is consistent: documentation, additional living expense, and smoke are all getting more attention. Documentation keeps widening. Pre-loss condition, evacuation timelines, and access constraints increasingly need to live in the file. The contemporaneous note discipline good adjusters already practice is closer to the baseline than the extra mile. ALE is under closer review. Additional living expense windows get examined more carefully, especially on extended-displacement losses. The partners handling this well treat the ALE clock as part of the file from day one, not an afterthought. Smoke claims are getting more attention. How smoke and residue damage is assessed and documented is a common friction point — and a common source of reopens when it&#x27;s handled thinly at first inspection. None of this is dramatic on its own. Cumulatively, it raises the standard on every California wildfire file. If you haven&#x27;t refreshed your wildfire workflow in the last year, this is a good moment. Most supplements aren&#x27;t surprises. They&#x27;re documentation failures that revealed themselves later. A supplement is what happens when a scope written in the field couldn&#x27;t carry the full story of the loss. The damage was always there. The note wasn&#x27;t. This is why contemporaneous documentation matters more than any process diagram a partner can show you. The scope that captures adjacent damage at first inspection produces a settlement that closes. The one that misses it produces a supplement, a delay, a call from a frustrated policyholder, and a carrier dollar paid twice. The work to prevent supplements is done before the adjuster leaves the property. Photos taken with intent. Measurements logged, not estimated. Adjacent areas walked, not assumed. It&#x27;s not glamorous. It&#x27;s also the difference between a file that closes once and a file that closes three times. The adjuster who opens a file often isn&#x27;t the one who closes it — and that handoff is one of the most reliable predictors of quality problems downstream. When an adjuster picks up a partially-worked file from a colleague who&#x27;s rotated out, two things happen. They rebuild context the cycle time number never accounts for. And the memory of the original inspection — the judgment calls, the coverage questions considered, the useful thing the policyholder mentioned — usually gets lost in the handoff. The carriers running cleaner files tend to work with partners who keep mid-deployment turnover low. Not zero — that&#x27;s unrealistic — but low. Three things worth checking on your next partner review: How many of your files changed adjusters between intake and closure last quarter? Of those, how many produced a supplement or reopen within 90 days? Is your partner reporting on adjuster continuity at all — or is that data sitting in their system, never surfacing? The partners who manage this well usually run a single point of contact that holds the file&#x27;s continuity even when the field adjuster changes. The ones who don&#x27;t, don&#x27;t. Do you actually know how many of your files changed hands last quarter? #ClaimsOperations #PropertyClaims #ClaimsManagement

Texas has been tightening scrutiny on appraisal — how it&#x27;s triggered, how appraisers are selected, what happens when the process stalls, and how awards get documented. The operational implications for adjusters working Texas property files are clearer now than they were even six months ago. A file that closes fast and reopens later costs more than one that closes a little slower and stays closed. Most CAT scorecards can&#x27;t see that. Carriers measure CAT response on a short list of numbers — cycle time, files closed per week, deployment speed, cost per file. They tell you how quickly a partner moved through volume. They don&#x27;t tell you what the volume actually cost. Here&#x27;s the problem. A file that closes quickly and reopens months later is not the same file as one that closes a bit slower and stays closed. The first one costs more. The cost just lands in a different column of the P&L, well after the deployment dashboard everyone watched during the event has gone quiet. Most carriers never run that math all the way out. Why the cost stays hidden Cycle time gets reported during the deployment. Supplements and reopens get reported much later — by which point the event that produced them is closed in everyone&#x27;s mind. Different teams own different numbers. The CAT team owns deployment speed. Claims operations owns reopen rates. Each optimizes for the number it&#x27;s accountable to, and nobody runs the full-cycle cost that ties them together. Cost per file gets logged at close, not at final resolution. A file that closes, then reopens for more, shows up as two smaller numbers in two different reports — never as the single, larger true cost. None of this is anyone&#x27;s fault. It&#x27;s a measurement problem. The dashboard was built for visibility during the event, not for full-lifecycle cost. What changes when you measure it properly The partners with the fastest cycle times aren&#x27;t always the most cost-effective. Speed without process discipline produces files that don&#x27;t stay closed, and the cost shows up later. The partners who run a little slower but reopen far less are often the better commercial decision. The math doesn&#x27;t work in the obvious direction — it works in the direction the data actually supports. And the pressure points worth fixing usually sit upstream of the field: intake routing, coverage decision authority, and documentation standards that hold under surge. Those move total cost more than any speed gain in the field can. What I&#x27;d suggest after this season Look at each major event two ways: not just how fast files closed, but how many quietly reopened after they did. Put those side by side and the real story shows up. Then ask which partners had the smallest gap between how fast they closed and what the file finally cost. Those are the operations actually delivering what the speed numbers seem to promise. Claims leadership is going to get measured differently over the next decade — by full lifecycle cost, the way reinsurers and regulators are already moving. The partners who thrive in that world are the ones built for total cost, not for the dashboard number that runs during the event. Fast looks cheap. It usually isn&#x27;t. So before the next hurricane season, one question: are you measuring what your CAT response cost during the event, or what it cost once every file finally stopped moving? — Mark Vetrovec CEO, Aspen Claims Service
